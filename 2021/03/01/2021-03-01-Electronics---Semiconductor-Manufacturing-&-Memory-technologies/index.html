Semiconductor Manufacturing: Silicon → Wafers 3:41

From silicon ore to silicon wafers

Reduction of quartzite (SiO2) 5:38

Submerged electrode arc furnace

Reduction to ~98% pure Si (mostly Al & Fe impurities)

SiC(s) + SiO2(s) → Si(l) + SiO(g) + CO(g)

Purification of silicon 8:40

Metallurgical grade Si → Electronic grade Si

  • Fractional distillation process:

    Si(s) +3HCl(g) → SiHCl3(g) +H2(g) +heat

  • Fluidized bed reactor @300°C:

    SiHCl3(g) +H2(g) → Si(s) +3HCl(g)

  • Can be looped to increase purity to less than 1 part per 109.

Making polycrystalline Si into a monocrystalline Si 11:44

Starting from a seed crystal

Main used technique: Czochralski crystal growth method (>1412°C)

  • Suited for large Si wafers but may have some oxygen inclusion impurities.

Starting from a seed crystal

Alternative technique: Float zone technique

  • Very high purity but small wafers. Used for detectors, power devices, expensive high-efficiency solar panels (spacecraft). Also, highly transparent to THz waves.

Wafer shaping & cutting 18:06

Largest wafers used today: 300mm diameter, ~775µm thick

  • Remove both ends of ingot
  • Surface grinding, edge bevelling
  • Wafer cutting with industrial grade diamond.

Semiconductor manufacturing: Wafers → Integrated circuits

Clean rooms 21:41

Design & contamination control

  • Modern IC chips have transistors in the 10nm range → sub-µm particles may damage or destroy parts of chips → cleanroom to minimize contaminants
  • More contaminants means lower wafer yield, performance (lifetime) and reliability
  • ISO cleanroom standards: maximum number of particles/volume bigger than some threshold

Airborne contaminants wide range of gaseous contaminants (sulfate, nitrate, organics, …) Wafer transfer, chemical delivery system … Wafer transfer, chemical delivery system …
Bacteria All kind Humans, improperly disinfected materials and equipment
Chemicals Chlorine Lithography, etching, cleaning products, …
Metal ions Sodium (ppm – ppb range) Electrically active impurities in wafers from processed silicon

Main categories of IC device manufacturing 27:53

Layering: growing and deposition

Layering: growing techniques 29:49

Consumes underlying silicon for forming new layer

Oxidation: Si + O2 → SiO2

Nitration: 3Si + 2N2 → Si3N4

  • Electrical isolation (oxide layer is superior)
  • Oxide layer acts as the active gate in MOSFET
  • Protective layer, passivation (nitride is superior), T resistance
  • Used for patterning
  • Variants: “wet” and “dry” oxidation

Layering: deposition techniques 34:28

Does not consume silicon, new layer is deposited

  • homo/hetero epitaxy
  • Silicon films: epitaxy layer, polysilicon
  • Dielectric Films: SiO2, Nitride, …
  • Metals: aluminum, tungsten, TiN, …

  • Used for Ga & As
  • Very precise composition can be obtained
  • Can be used for doping too
  • Used for quantum nanostructures

Depositing of thin metallic layers for

  • Contact metals in thin-film transistors
  • Hard disk production
  • Antireflection coating in optics

  • Inexpensive equipment → relatively cheap
  • Relatively uniform and compact deposits, but not as much as CVD or sputtering
  • Generally use for layers > 1µm

Lithography & Etching

Patterning or Lithography 44:41

Selectively mask or expose parts of the wafer

  • Determines the critical dimensions of the device (now 10nm & 7nm)
  • Highly defect sensitive (pattern creation & alignment)
  • Reticle: hard copy of design → made with laser writing or electron beam
  • Developing-expose through mask; first phase is reversible
  • Photoresists, positive & negative → solubility

Etching: removal of top layers from the wafer 51:11

Typically in conjunction with lithography

  • Wet etching: wafers are immersed in tank of etchant (e.g. KOH)
  • Dry etching: exposure to ion bombardment for dislodging surface material

Doping: making extrinsic semiconductors 55:11

Achieving specific dopant concentration & distribution, creating junctions

  • Thermal diffusion (impurity source can be solid, liquid or gaseous)
    • High T: lithography can’t be used → SiO2 layer
    • Lateral spread within the material; solubility limit
  • Ion implantation
    • Around room T, conventional lithography possible
    • Leads to damage of wafer surface → post annealing

Memory technologies

The Memory hierarchy in a computer 1:09:43

Optimizing cost and speed

Static random-access memory 1:13:45

Properties:

  • Volatile
  • Bits are stored as on/off switches
  • No charges to leak
  • No refreshing needed when powered
  • More complex construction
  • Larger per bit
  • More expensive
  • Faster
  • Primarily used for caching
  • Digital → uses flip-flops

Operation

Dynamic random-access memory 1:24:20

Properties：

  • Volatile
  • Bits stored as charge in capacitors
  • Charges leak → Need to refresh circuits periodically
  • Simpler construction
  • Smaller per bit
  • Less expensive
  • Slower
  • Main memory (Computer “RAM”)
  • Essentially analogue → charge level determines value

Flash memory 1:33:52

Introduction

Flash memory stores information in an array of memory cells made from floating gate transistors.

  • Non-volatile
  • Traditional single-level cell (SLC) devices: 1 bit/cell
  • Newer multi-level cell (MLC) devices can store multiple bits/cell by choosing between multiple levels of charge.

Reading of a single cell

Writing of cells: tunnelling

NOR Flash memory

NAND Flash memory

NOR vs NAND flash

  • NOR Flash

    • Faster to read than NAND

    • Slower to erase & write

    • Larger cells

    • More expensive

      → Use in mobile devices, tablets, …

  • NAND Flash

    • Slower to read than NOR

    • Faster to erase & write

    • Much smaller cells

    • Less expensive

      → High memory density

      → Good for SD cards, Digital cameras, USB sticks

Hard Disk Drives (HDD) 1:49:55

Introduction

  • Magnetic storage
  • About 90% of data centre storage

Basic operation

  • Reading head floats only a few nm above the ferromagnetic film of the disk platter.
  • Current in write head induces magnetic field → alters bits
  • Read element will detects voltage changes, induced by motion over the magnetic field.
  • Transition to perpendicular recording in ~2005 → more magnetically stable, stronger fields → boost in density
  • In modern HDDs take up less than 50 nm/bit

Helium sealed drives

  • Drag on rotating platters scales as 𝜌𝑣3.
  • Helium is ~7 less dense than air + has better thermal conductivity
    • Reduction in power
    • Lower turbulence
    • Better cooling
  • More thinner platters possible → more capacity
  • Introduced to the market in 2013

Challenge: the superparamagnetic effect

  • Individual grains are only ~8nm in diameter

  • Physical limit - the superparamagnetic effect: magnetization can randomly flip direction under the influence of temperature.

  • Current materials do not allow to go below ~7nm grains.

  • Coercivity is a measure of the ability of a ferromagnetic material to withstand an external magnetic field without becoming demagnetized.

  • Higher coercivity means higher stability, but also harder to write to → new writing technologies needed.

  • Microwave-Assisted Magnetic Recording (MAMR) uses 20-40 GHz frequencies to bombard the disk platter with a circular microwave field, lowering its coercivity and enabling the bit value to be written.

  • Heat-Assisted Magnetic Recording (HAMR) uses a tiny 200 mW laser beam to heat up the disk platter to 400 °C and permit the writing operation.

  • Heated-Dot Magnetic Recording (HDMR) extends HAMR, patterning the disk into small “dots”. This allows to better focus the laser energy on individual dots.

  • Expected 10x gains in density over current HDDs on the market → >100 TB HDDs
