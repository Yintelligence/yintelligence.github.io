Data analytics and connected care in low resource settings

Challenges of data analytics in LRS

Descriptive analytics

  • Structured aggregate data (first step of data reduction)
  • Descriptive statistics (mean, median, variance, skewing, outliers)
  • 90% of today’s data analytics
  • Challenges in LRS health care:
    • Quantitative: unstructured paper based primary data sources
    • Qualitative: parallel aggregate data collection (lack of motivation)

Predictive analytics

  • Probabilistic forecasting of events
  • Predictive modelling
    • what can happen if I give treatment X?
  • Root cause analysis
    • what are the signs and symptoms related to long length of stay in case of treatment X?
  • Data mining
    • which symptoms are most related to disease X?)
  • Forecasting
    • what happens if the covid-19 reproduction rate continues to grow at the actual pace?
  • Monte-Carlo simulations
    • what are the treatments suggested by a care guideline based on any subset of a list of symptoms?
  • Pattern detection
    • which are the combinations of clinical conditions that relate to high in-hospital mortality rates?
  • Challenges in LRS health care:
    • Probabilistic ≠ explanatory
    • Tunnel vision

Clinical decision support

  • Identify data uncertainties to make better decisions (e.g. Ikirezi panorama)
  • Optimize treatment in order to achieve better outcomes (e.g. Ikirezi clinical pathways, Ikirezi pharmaceutics)
  • Risk and survival probability assessment (e.g. triage protocols & ICU guidance)
  • Challenges in LRS care:
    • Exaggerated confidence → blunting of clinical reasoning (nursing staff)
    • Evidence based medicine ≠ national care programs and guidelines
    • Accountability

Diagnostic analytics

  • Explanation of causal relationships
    • E.g. why do patients under treatment with drug X have a longer length of stay compared to drug Y?
  • Requires huge etiological, pathophysiological, pharmacologic etc. and very costly knowledge bases
    • Translational medicine
    • Drug discovery
    • Clinical trials
    • Out of scope for LRS today

Connected care and systems interoperability in LRS

Lots of challenges

  • Poor connectivity infrastructure (intranet, internet; Africa: < 22% coverage)
  • Chaotic and organic growth of isolated specialized subsystems
    • Often disease related (malaria, HIV, TB, maternal health…Covid-19)
    • Mostly donor driven (budget driven priorities, short time frames, redundancy)
  • Computer illiteracy (care providers, patients)
  • Lack of local IT support
  • Lack of international standards (e.g. DHIS-2 implementations, ontologies)
  • Lack of data security and privacy protection measures
  • Unprepared legal systems

NEEDED FOR PROVIDING QUALITY SOURCE DATA IN SUFFICIENT QUANTITIES FOR ALL TYPES OF DATA ANALYTICS

Notable efforts

  • Accessibility of internet connectivity (e.g. Starlink, Mesh++)
  • Health information broadcasting (mostly SMS, targeted patient groups, e.g. Kira Mama, RapidSMS)
  • Regional health facility networks (e.g. KHIN, regional PACS implementations)
  • Standards based open source health software projects (OpenClinic, OpenMRS, GNU Health, Ikirezi)
  • WHO & ITU digital health leadership capacity building
    • National digital health strategic planning (WHO/ITU toolkit)
    • Development of national e-health enterprise architectures (TOGAF, WHO/ITU)
  • International digital health education programs harmonization (EAC, francophone countries)
  • Results based financing programs with clinical documentation performance indicators

Clinical decision support and the Ikirezi program

The Ikirezi program

Clinical Decision Support Systems (CDSS)

  • provide clinicians at the right time with information describing a patient’s clinical condition and appropriate knowledge applicable to it, filtered and presented appropriately for improving the quality of care and the patient’s health status.

Ikirezi ambition

  • offer clinical decision support solutions at and beyond the point of care, adapted to the context of health systems in low resource countries

Characteristics

  • Pragmatism: focus on severe and treatable health conditions and avoidable health risks
  • Free and open source software
  • Widespread common technologies (java based)
  • Integration with existing popular health information systems (DHIS2, OpenClinic)
  • Academic support (content validation, training, research)

Opportunities

  • Optimization of cost/efficiency of health interventions
  • Reduction of health risks
  • Improved quality of care
  • Clinical documentation completeness
  • Operationalization of evidence-based medicine

Limitations

  • Clinical decision support tools: the health professional remains entirely responsible for the diagnostic and therapeutic decision he makes
  • Limited information access: drugs, allergies, complaints, symptoms and signs, diagnostics, lab results, digital images…
  • No pathophysiological or etiological explanation

Standardized clinical language

  • Medical knowledge formalized in standardized language (ontology)
    • Using international classification systems and nomenclatures
    • Anatomic Therapeutic Classification (WHO)
    • Normalized Names for Clinical Drugs – RxNorm (NLM)
    • International Classification of Diseases - ICD10 (WHO)
    • International Classification for Primary Care - ICPC2 (WONCA)
  • Large knowledge base with associative probabilities for ontology categories: diseases, signs, symptoms and drugs (incidences and prevalence)

INTERACTION WITH IKIREZI THROUGH ONTOLOGY CATEGORIES AND THEIR PROPERTIES

Anatomical Therapeutic Classification (ATC)

  • WHO
  • Anatomical group (1 letter), main pharmacological group(2 figures), chemical or pharmacological subgroup (2 letters), chemical substance (2 figures)
  • Example:
    • A. Digestive system and metabolism
    • A01. Stomatology preparations
    • A01AA .Prophylactic agents against caries
    • A01AA01 Sodium Fluoride

Normalized Names for Clinical Drugs (RxNorm)

  • US National Library of Medicine
  • Unique number for every pharmaceutical product form on the US market
  • Links with other pharmaceutical databases such as ATC, DrugBank, Gold Standard, Micromedex, Medi-Span...
  • Numerical code
    • Ex: 161 = Paracetamol/Acetominophen

International Classification of Diseases (ICD10)

  • WHO
  • 14.400 codes for diseases, signs, symptoms, social conditions and external causes of injury.
    • Normalized disease definitions
    • Clear inclusion/exclusion criteria
  • Ex. B50 = Malaria due to Plasmodium Falciparum

International Classification for Primary Care (ICPC-2)

  • WONCA , WHO
  • Coding of 3 different categories of items in primary care (2.300 codes)
    • Reason for encounter
    • Diagnosed health problems
    • Care procedures
    • Inclusion & exclusion criteria
  • Ex. W05 = Nausea/vomiting during pregnancy

Coding aid

  • 3BT clinical thesaurus
    • Clinical concepts table mapping (categories and category clusters) onto common language tokens (keywords)
    • Translations in French, English, Spanish and Dutch
    • Multi-classification links (ICD-10, ICPC-2, DSM-4)
      • OpenClinic
  • Terminology translation services
    • Most knowledge bases only available in English
    • Automated translation through IATE/EURODICATOM (InterActive Terminology for Europe)
      • OpenClinic

Internet (in)dependency

  • International knowledge bases can be queried through RESTful web services
    • Not useful and impossible to make local copies of knowledge bases
    • Permanent updates
    • Very large data volumes (terabytes)
    • 95% of the knowledge not applicable to low resource settings
  • Local cache memory
    • Temporary storage of retrieved knowledge for a limited time (3 months)
    • Background update processes
    • Queries served from local cache when matching search criteria
    • Local knowledge store accessible when internet connectivity is down

Part 1: Diagnostic decision support – cluster hypothesis approach

1 or 2 main complaints as a starting point

  • Avoid tunnel vision
  • Standards based extraction of Ikirezi ontology categories (complaints and reasons for encounter) from existing medical record content
  • Adding of new complaints possible at any time

Diagnostic panorama view

  • Focus on severe and treatable diseases
  • Suggests relevant signs and symptoms for each disease with their confirming and excluding powers
  • Dynamic diagnostic completeness calculation based on power table
  • Possibility to view full panorama signs and symptoms from power table
  • Selected knowledge elements automatically transferred to medical record

Example

  • Main complaints (since 2 days)
    • Edema of the ankles
    • High grade fever
  • Additional signs
    • Proteinuria

Discussion

What opportunities and risks can be associated to a cluster hypothesis approach CDSS?

Part 2: Diagnostic decision support – Bayesian model approach

  • All available signs and symptoms are simultaneously considered

  • Dynamic calculation of probabilities of all ontology item associations

    • Bayes rule \[ Pr(A|B)=Pr(A)×Pr(B|A)/Pr(B) \]

    • Pure probabilistic approach

      • Prevalence
      • Disease expression probabilities (associated symptoms)
      • Incidences and waiting room probabilities

    • Can suggest relevant missing signs and symptoms

    • Close interaction with electronic medical record

Example

Exercise

  • Female adult patient

  • Main complaints: fever, edema of the ankles

  • Lab: proteinuria

  • At the emergency department: convulsions & coma

  • Diagnosis?

Comparing cluster hypothesis to Bayesian approach in Ikirezi

  • Cluster hypothesis approach (prescriptive)
    • Clinical examination completeness (identifies data uncertainties)
    • Diagnostics not to be missed (severe and treatable, risk assessment)
    • Based on disease expression probabilities (signs and symptoms) limited to main complaints
  • Bayesian approach (predictive)
    • Disease probability calculation based on signs and symptoms (pattern recognition)
    • Based on disease expression (signs and symptoms) probabilities of all available signs and symptoms

Part 3: Therapeutic decision support

Drug-drug interactions (prescriptive: outcome optimization)

  • Ex. Aspirin – Heparin
  • Can be detected at different levels
    • Drug prescriber
    • Drug dispensing
    • Patient record consultation
    • Clinical assistance invocation
  • Pharmaceutical category associations
    • Contra-indications
      • Ex. Aspirin – Stomach ulcer
    • Indications
      • Ex. Aspirin – Headache
    • Allergies and cross-allergies
      • Exc. Macrolides - Erythromycin

Example

  • Prescription
    • Quinine tablet 300mg
    • Fluconazole capsule 50mg
    • Omeprazole tablet 20mg
  • Prescription
    • Aspiring 500mg
    • Heparin 5000IU
    • Patient with history of gastric ulcer

Discussion

  • Knowledge filtering and presentation
  • Is there a need to warn about all known drug-drug interactions and contra-indications possible?

Part 4: Specific decision support tools (triage)

  • Pediatric triage according to Canadian Pediatric Triage and Acuity Scale (CPTAS) (Prescriptive: risk & survival probability assessment)
    • Standardization of care priority scores
    • Standardization of surveillance strategy
  • Covid-19 triage record based on WHO guidelines
    • Taking into account limited testing capabilities
    • Triage schemas based on diagnostic instrument availability (pulseoxymeter)
  • Simple and fast procedures: timely filtered knowledge presentation
    • First assessment triangle
    • Evaluation of the reason for encounter and associated elements
    • Calculation of treatment priority

Part 5: Clinical pathways and patient treatment guidelines

Introduction

  • Objectives
    • Rationalization of patient care
    • Standardization of competence and training levels
    • Optimal use of limited health resources
    • Standardization of treatments
    • Formalization of referral criteria
  • Foundations
    • Evidence based medicine
    • Health economics data
    • National health strategies (priorities)
  • Target audience
    • First level health facilities
    • Care providers with weak diagnostic and therapeutic competences

Utilization

  • Triage (fast and standardized care decisions)
  • Less common diseases
  • Health costs control
  • Supply chain optimization
  • Patient referral criteria
  • Routine health information reporting
  • Simple and robust user interfaces (smartphone, tablet)
  • Portable XML formatted knowledge representation (common knowledge base for different development teams

Case study: SPT in Burundi

  • 48 priority health complaints defined as starting points
  • Coverage of 200+ priority diseases
  • Parallel decision tree approach
  • Example
    • 19 year old women
    • Complaints
      • Cough, mainly at night
      • Short of breath
    • Additional data
      • Wheezing, quickly out of breath, fatigue, bad mood

Takeaways

  • No “one fits all” CDSS
    • Decision support instruments must be adapted to existing knowledge gaps and information needs
    • Personal ROI critical for CDSS adoption (certainly in low resource settings)
  • Fading difference between user and instrument: human factor of resistance
    • Care provider becomes the sensor
    • CDSS take over part of the clinical reasoning
    • Bright future for more sophisticated AI methods: dynamic knowledge gathering
  • New applications of CDSS integrated in HIMS
    • Knowledge gaps identification guiding future training interventions
    • Permanent quality control related to Results Based Financing programs
    • Continued Professional Education
