Standards in EMR

Video

Many of the issues raised last rime could be solved when info is “encoded according to some standard”. But what does this mean?

The need for standardisation 13:00

Two aspects:

  • Encoding the information in a uniform way
    • Code 12345 -> heart disease
  • Standardizing the way we exchange information between centers
    • MSH|^~&||OTHER REG MED … -> A female patient aged X visited me on date Y and ….

Healthcare Standards can improve patient care

An enabler for interoperability of electronic healthcare information

  • Improve quality of care
  • Electronics documents provide value to clinicians
  • Ensure clinicians have latest knowledge
  • Improve patient safety
  • Minimize preventable errors
  • Lower cost of healthcare delivery
  • Eliminate duplicate medical tests
  • Support lifetime of HER
  • Improve public health reporting
  • Empower patients to manage their own health
  • …

Interoperability and information exchange

Remember:

  • N different devices with bilateral communication: N(N-1) / 2 interfaces to maintain and specify; N(N-1) / 2 connections to maintain
  • N different devices in a star: N interfaces, N connections

HL7: for exchanging information 16:14

The need:

  • Obvious need to move data around between applications

  • Apps written by different vendors

  • Vendors create apps “in the vacuum”

  • Data models differ

  • Application uses differ

“HL7 International is one of several American National Standards Institute (ANSI) accredited Standards Developing Organizations (SDOs) operating in the healthcare arena. “

“HL7 provides standards for interoperability that improve care delivery, optimize workflow, reduce ambiguity and enhance knowledge transfer among all of our stakeholders, including healthcare providers, government agencies, the vendor community, fellow SDOs and patients.

In all of our processes we exhibit timeliness, scientific rigor and technical expertise without compromising transparency, accountability, practicality, or our willingness to put the needs of our stakeholders first.”

HL7 is consensus driven 18:08

  • Volunteer driven
  • So, heterogeneous group, with different interests, commitment and understanding
    • Governments
    • Vendors
    • Clinicians
    • Other (Inter)national standards organisations (ANSI, ISO, …
  • Meetings 3 times a year
  • Slow process
  • Consensus driven implies compromises need to be made
  • Hence sometimes ambiguous

Interoperability 26:00

“The ability of two or more systems or components to exchange information and to use the information that has been exchanged” (IEEE Standard Computer Dictionary)

So, interoperability requires standards for

  • Exchanging information: describing syntax
  • Using the information: describing semantics

HL7: actually a family of standards 28:39

Sharing and re-use of information within and between hospitals

  • Patient Administration and demographics
  • Orders and results for: Clinical Lab/Pathology, Imaging (radiology, us),..
  • Signs and symptoms, diagnosis and treatments
  • Pharmacy prescriptions, dispensing and administration
  • Patient care messages, clinical documents
  • Claims and reimbursements
  • Scheduling and managing healthcare resources
  • Clinical research and public health surveillance
  • …

HL7: why 7? 29:24

  • Open Systems Interconnection (OSI) reference model

  • Technology standard from International Standards Organisation (ISO)

  • 1984

  • Abstract model of how network protocols and equipment should interoperate

The OSI Application level

“The application level addresses definition of the data to be exchanged, the timing of the interchange, and the communication of certain errors to the application. The seventh level supports such functions as security checks, participant identification, availability checks, exchange mechanism negotiations and, most importantly, data exchange structuring.”

HL7 version 2.x 32:12

Aims:

  • To improve the electronic exchange of demographic, and immunization records to State IIS (registries) from eligible providers and hospitals
  • To improve the ability of eligible hospital laboratories to send reportable data to public health agencies
  • To improve the electronic exchange of relevant syndromic surveillance data between healthcare providers and public health agencies

Not aiming for:

  • Security/Access Control – HL7 does not provide for the enforcement of a user’s security policies. In addition, HL7 does not specify a specific encryption method.
  • Privacy/Confidentiality – HL7 does not address this issue and makes no assumption about how the data will be used at the source or destination of a message.
  • Accountability/Audit trails – HL7 does not attempt to define possible transaction processing features needed in a user’s environment.

Clinical Document Architecture (CDA) 46:35

  • International standard from HL7

  • Standardization of clinical documents for storage and exchange

  • XML based

  • Header is consistent across all clinical documents

    • identifies and classifies the document, provides information on patient,provider, encounter, and authentication

  • Body contains narrative text / multimedia content (level 1),optionally augmented by coded equivalents (levels 2&3)

  • CDA is being seen by many as the primary format for diagnostic reports and medical summaries

    • Allows for a smooth transition from free text to coded content
    • Can reference external documents such as images, signals, films
    • Easy to display (simple style sheet)

  • Only a content format, no definition of services or messages

    • many options: HL7 v2, HL7 v3, FTP, E-Mail, HTTP, SOAP, etc.
    • but no real integration of document and communication protocol

FHIR 47:50

  • Fast (to design and implement)
  • Health
  • Interoperable
  • Resources

Why fhir is better (according to fhir)? 48:32

FHIR offers many improvements over existing standards:

  • A strong focus on implementation – fast and easy to implement (multiple developers have had simple interfaces working in a single day)
  • Multiple implementation libraries, many examples available to kick-start development
  • Specification is free for use with no restrictions
  • Interoperability out-of-the-box– base resources can be used as is, but can also be adapted for local requirements
  • Evolutionary development path from HL7 Version 2 and CDA – standards can co-exist and leverage each other
  • Strong foundation in Web standards– XML, JSON, HTTP, Atom, OAuth, etc.
  • Support for RESTful architectures and also seamless exchange of information using messages or documents
  • Concise and easily understood specifications
  • A Human-readable wire format for ease of use by developers
  • Solid ontology-based analysis with a rigorous formal mapping for correctness

SNOMED CT 58:47

Systematized Nomenclature of Medicine: Clinical Terms

SNOMED CT is a reference terminology for clinical terms and consists of terms used in health and health care that range from abscess to zygote that can code contents belonging to all parts of a clinical record within the Subjective-Objective-Assessment-Plan (SOAP) paradigm.

A code system that enables machines to “interpret” clinical record contents.

22298006 means “myocardial infarc5on (MI)”

399211009 means “past history of MI”

  • Dates back to 1965, when Systematized Nomenclature of Pathology (SNOP) was published.

  • Is released/updated twice a year

    • New drugs, therapies, …

  • SNOMED CT organizes concepts in a hierarchy: from general to specific

  • Each concept contains:

    • a unique concept identifier.
    • A human readable description
    • A fully specified name (FSN)

  • 400K concepts, 1.2M descriptions, 2.4M links

Pros and Cons 1:25:28

Pros:

  • Machine readable concepts, hierarchies, links
  • Expressive power for expressing clinical reasoning
  • Multi language
  • Multiple inheritance

Cons:

  • Some problems have many different concepts, some areas are densely covered, others are not
  • Not possible to identify equivalence based on code automatically
  • Hard to learn
  • So large you cannot even read it properly

Others 1:30:14

  • ICD-9 US for diagnosis and procedures

    • 14K codes

    • Codes 3 to 5 digits

    • Again a single hierarchy, under debate

  • ICD-10

    • 69K codes
    • Codes up to 7 digits
    • Again a single hierarchy, under debate

  • Added laterality

  • Some mappings from SNOMED CT to ICD10

  • ICD11 as a unifier?

Recap

  • Snomed CT and ICD9 and beyond as standard terminologies

  • HL7 as a standard for information exchange

  • There are problems with what they specify

  • There are problems with what they do not specify

  • There are problems in making people use the standards in a proper way
