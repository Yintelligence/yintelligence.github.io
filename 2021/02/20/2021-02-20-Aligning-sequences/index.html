Why alignment? 28:30

The problem 30:02

Comparison may be difficult due to

Look for the alignment that produces the maximum similarity

Some definitions 31:40

Identity

The level of identity is the fraction of residues which are identical between two sequences

  • The identity between two randomly generated sequences is never 0%

Similarity

Two residues are similar when their substitution has no effect on the functionality of the protein. The level of similarity is the fraction of residues that are similar

Homology

Two sequences are homologous when the similarity between them is derived from a common ancestor

  • Homology is a binary relationship

Why the maximum similarity? 34:44

If we have two genes or proteins that come from the same common ancestor, we want the amino acids, which are part of this common ancestor, to align properly

Attention !!! 38:19

Homology implies a common ancestor and at the same time it may mean that both sequences have a similar structure or function

But this is not always the case:

Mutation may result in sequences with different functions or some protein sequences may be completely different, but still perform the same function or two sequences may be similar but have no common ancestor

One always has to be careful and look for additional information to make sure the claim sticks

Since the common ancestor is often not known, an alignment is just an hypothesis about a relationship between two sequences

A quantitative method is required that takes into account the changes accepted by evolution

A quantitative score 46:12

The idea is that a quantitative method provides a higher score to the alignment of sequences that are indeed derived from a common ancestor than between sequences that are related by chance

The best score is for the exact alignment = Optimal alignment

There is currently no quantitative method that uses a complete and exact model of evolution

The best score is therefore not necessarily the correct alignment (in terms of a common ancestor)

  • These methods are used to look for similar (MAX) or different (MIN) sequences

Example 47:29

Basic idea 49:23

There are two models that can explain the similarities and differences between two sequences

  • a random model
  • a non-random model (evolutionary)

When the probability of a match between the sequences is determined, one can decide which of the two models is more likely

Random model 50:09

In this model there are no constraints on the sequence composition

Meaning that the choice of each residue is independent from the other residues in the sequence

  • The probability depends on the frequency of every amino acid in the population (= all characters in the sequences)
  • When the frequency of the amino acids a is pa, the probability of having a at that position is also pa

When two residues a and b are aligned, the probability of the match is pa pb

Non-random (or evolutionary) mode 51:50

This model proposes that the sequences are related because of common descent

  • This implies that there are constraints on the composition of the sequence

The probability of a match depends on the residue at the same position in the common ancestor

  • This probability is qa,b
    • The actual value of qa,b depends on the evolutionary mechanism

When two residues a and b are aligned, the probability of a match is qa,b since the residues are correlated

Basic idea 53:12

The odds ratio qa,b/papb shows which model is more likely

  • When qa,b/papb >1 the evolutionary model explains better the alignment

The model has to explain the complete alignment

  • All odds ratio’s need to be combined

It is often easier to use sums instead of products

The log-odds ratio is the value that is written in a substitution matrix

Substitution matrices 1:03:13

Two methods have been used to determine the values of all Sa,b

  • All PAM matrices were derived from one manually constructed matrix (PAM1)
    • 71 groups of manually aligned protein sequences (no gaps) were used and in each group there was an identity of at least 85% between each sequence

  • Every BLOSUM matrix was constructed from a clustered set of highly conserved blocks of amino acids
    • The clustering was also based on identity, meaning that each sequence in each cluster has at least x% identity
    • In case of BLOSUM62, this is 62% identity

PAM matrices 1:05:23

The idea was to create a similarity distance measure

Which requires the definition of a unit of distance

  • PAM1 = 1 mutation accepted for 100 amino acids

  • PAM120 = 120 mutations accepted for 100 amino acids

    (multiple mutations at same position possible!)

Substitution matrices

The PAM matrices, build by Margaret Dayhoff, provide a similarity score between amino acids

When two sequence have an identity of 40%, which matrix should we use ?

BLOSUM matrices were created by using blocks of conserved amino acids in clusters of sequences

Analysis of BLOSUM62 1:12:22

Substitution matrices

Which matrix should be used ????

To evaluate similar sequences one needs matrices that give high values voor identical matches : e.g. BLOSUM 80 or PAM50

If one is not sure it’s good to start with BLOSUM62 and PAM120

The matrix choice is biased by the percentage of conservation the biologist expects to find between the sequences

Which PAM or BLOSUM? 1:17:28

Using relative entropy (H) one can verify whether the matrix is still capable of making the distinction between the evolutionary and the random model

The gaps 1:18:04

Homologous sequences often have a different length

If one wants to align these to sequences in an optimal manner, gaps need to be introduced

One often makes the difference between the start of a gap (penalty of -10 and -15) or the extension of existing gaps (penalty of -0.5 or -2)

Different kinds of alignment 1:19:25

There are two big classes of alignment methods

  • Global alignment : The alignment between the complete sequences
    • Interesting to compare two related sequences
  • Local alignment : aligning of conserved regions in two different proteins
    • To detect for instance identical domains or motives

Global alignment 1:20:21

Dynamic programming to align two sequences by Saul Needleman and Christian Wunsch in 1970

The semiglobal alignment

Make the penalties on S(i,0) en S(0,j) equal to 0. in this way we remove the obligation that the sequences have to align at the start and the end of the sequences

All the previous methods used a linear penalty for the gaps in the alignment

g(ngap) = -ngapE linear gap penalty

As mentioned earlier, we an differentiate between starting a gap and extending one

g(ngap) = -I-(ngap-1) E Affine gap penalty

How does the DP algorithm change?

Do we open or extend a gap?

The algorithm can be adapted so that we obtain a solution in O(mn)
